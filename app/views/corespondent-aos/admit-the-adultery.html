How your answer will affect the divorce

The court will take your answer into account in its judgement on whether the adultery took place, and therefore whether there are grounds for divorce.

You don’t have to admit to the adultery if you don’t want to. You can allow the divorce to proceed (not ‘defend’ it) and not admit to the adultery.

If the applicant has claimed the divorce costs

In divorces based on adultery, the applicant can also claim the costs from the person who allegedly committed adultery with the respondent (known as the co-respondent).

The applicant's costs usually include the £550 fee. They could also include other court fees and costs. The costs could be much higher if the applicant has legal fees (eg a solicitor) and these are also included.

You may be liable to pay the costs regardless of whether or not you admit to the adultery. However, if you do admit to it and the applicant relies on it being proof that the adultery took place, then this may result in an order for costs being made against you. You should speak to a solicitor if you aren’t sure how to respond.

You will have a chance to respond to the costs claim later in this application. If you object to paying the costs you'll need to provide a reason for not paying them, and may need to attend a hearing to present your case.
