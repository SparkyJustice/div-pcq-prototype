Warning A costs order will only cover the divorce costs, and won't include any costs related to financial settlements.

Read more about costs orders

It's up to the court to decide how the costs will be split, but it will usually split the costs as follows (unless your {{ respsHusbandOrWife() }} accepts half the costs, or you provide a good enough reason to not pay anything).

2 years separation with consent

The court will usually make an order that the respondent pay half of the applicant's costs.

5 years separation

The court usually won't make a costs order. However, it could order the respondent to pay costs under some circumstances.

Behaviour, adultery, and desertion

The court will usually order the respondent to pay all of the applicant's costs, unless the applicant has asked for less. If the court decides that the respondent should also pay the applicant's legal costs, it will usually order that the respondent only pay what it judges to be a reasonable amount of these. However, under certain circumstances it could order you to pay more and the costs could be significantly higher.

What happens now

Your response will be sent to your {{ respsHusbandOrWife() }}. They'll be asked to confirm that they want to claim their costs. If you suggest paying half the costs they'll be given the opportunity to accept this.

The court will take all your circumstances into account when it makes its decision, including your conduct, and your {{ respsHusbandOrWife() }}'s conduct, before and during the divorce.

You'll be given the court's decision at the same time as you find out if the divorce will be granted.

Objecting to a costs order

After it's been made, you can dispute the costs order if you don't think that you should pay it. You'll need to send a letter to the court stating why you don't think you should pay at least 14 days before the hearing.
