What this means

The courts of England and Wales must have the jurisdiction (the legal power) to be able to grant a divorce. The applicant confirmed that the legal statement, or statements, given in the application apply to either, or both the applicant and respondent.

Each legal statement includes some or all of the following legal connections to England and Wales.

Habitual residence

If your lives are mainly based in England or Wales then you're what is legally known as 'habitually resident'.

This may include working, owning property, having children in school, and your main family life taking place in England or Wales.

The examples above aren't a complete list of what makes up habitual residence, and just because some of them apply to you doesn’t mean you’re habitually resident. If you’re not sure, you should get legal advice.

Domicile

Your domicile is usually the place in which you were born, regard as your permanent home and to which you have the closest ties.

However, domicile can be more complex if you or your parents have moved countries in the past.

When you're born, you acquire a domicile of origin. This is usually:

  • the country your father considered his permanent home if your parents were married
  • the country your mother considered her permanent home if your parents were unmarried, or your father had died

If you leave your domicile of origin and settle in another country as an adult, the new country may become your domicile of choice.

If you're not sure about your domicile you should get legal advice.

Residual jurisdiction

The court may have residual jurisdiction if none of the other connections apply, and both of the following are true:

  • either the applicant or the respondent are domiciled in England or Wales
  • neither the applicant nor the respondent are able to apply (on the basis of their nationality or residence) for a divorce in another member state of the EU

In addition, if the marriage is between a same-sex couple, the court may have residual jurisdiction if all the following apply:

  • the applicant and the respondent married each other in England or Wales
  • neither the applicant nor the respondent is able to apply for a divorce in any other country
  • it would be in the interests of justice for the court to consider the application (for example, this may apply if the applicant’s or respondent’s home country doesn’t allow divorce between same-sex couples).

However, residual jurisdiction can be more complex. If you’re not sure if this might apply to you, you should get legal advice.

What this means

The only legal ground for divorce in England and Wales is if the marriage has broken down irretrievably.

The applicant had to choose a reason for the breakdown of the marriage (legally known as the 'fact') from the following list.

Behaviour

The respondent has behaved in such a way that the applicant cannot reasonably be expected to live with them.

The applicant must provide enough examples of the respondent's behaviour to satisfy the court that they can't reasonably be expected to live with them. The examples must be of the respondent's behaviour, and examples where the applicant and respondent are both at fault cannot be used.

If the applicant lives with the respondent as a couple for more than 6 months after the most recent example of behaviour, the court will take this into account in its judgement of whether or not the marriage has broken down irretrievably. This could be over a single 6 month period, or if they live together on and off for several periods adding up to more than 6 months.

Adultery

The respondent committed adultery, and the applicant finds it intolerable to live with them.

Adultery can only be used if it was with a member of the opposite sex.

The applicant has the option of naming the person with whom the respondent committed adultery (known as the co-respondent). Co-respondents are sent a copy of the divorce application and asked if they want to defend the allegations.

Adultery can't be used as a reason if the applicant and respondent lived together as a couple for more than 6 months after the applicant found out about it. This could have been in one go, or if they lived together on and off for several periods of time adding up to more than 6 months.

Separated for 2 years with consent

The applicant and respondent must have spent the past 2 years living apart to use this reason for divorce.

They are allowed to have spent some time living together, but only for up to 6 months (over one or more periods) and this time doesn’t count towards the 2 year period. They’ll need to have lived apart for longer to make up any time that they spent living together.

The respondent must give their consent to a divorce for this reason to be used.

Separated for 5 years

The applicant and respondent must have spent the past 5 years living apart to use this reason for divorce.

They are allowed to have spent some time living together, but only for up to 6 months (over one or more periods) and this time doesn’t count towards the 5 year period. They’ll need to have lived apart for longer to make up any time that they spent living together.

Desertion

The respondent left the applicant without their agreement at least 2 years ago, and they have lived apart since.

The applicant and respondent are allowed to have spent some time living together since the desertion, but only for up to 6 months (over one or more periods) and this time doesn’t count towards the 2 year period. They’ll need to have lived apart for longer to make up any time that they spent living together.

The respondent must have chosen to leave the applicant. For example, it can't be used if they have been sent to prison.
