If you disagree with your {{ petsHusbandOrWife() }} about how your property, money, pensions and other assets will be split, then you can ask the court to decide for you. This includes maintenance payments.

These decisions are called 'financial orders'. You can apply for orders for yourself, and/or, if appropriate, for your children.

If you both agree on how to split your money and property and want to make it legally binding, you can apply for a type of financial order called a 'consent order'.

Read more about orders

Getting a consent order

Get a solicitor to draft the consent order, and then send it to the court with a £50 fee. Your consent order can only be approved once you get your decree nisi.

Applying for a financial order

To formally start legal proceedings, you'll also need to complete another form (known as Form A) and pay a fee of £255.

This means that if you select yes to the following question:

  • you don't have to proceed with the financial order
  • you can proceed with the financial order at any time in the future while your {{ petsHusbandOrWife() }} is alive

If you submit Form A then you can only get your Decree Absolute (the legal document ending your marriage) once the financial order has been made.

If you don't apply for a financial order now

If you select no, you can still apply for a financial order in future, but you’ll be more limited in that you can only do so until you remarry or form a civil partnership. This rule doesn’t apply to pension sharing or pension compensation sharing orders which can be applied for anytime.

You should get legal advice if you're not sure what to do.

Orders for child maintenance

The court can only make financial orders for children under certain circumstances. You'll usually either need to come to an agreement with your {{ petsHusbandOrWife() }} (which you can then make legally binding with a consent order) or use the Child Maintenance Service.
